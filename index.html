What Makes a Pokémon Good?¶

By Nicholas Levin

Ever since the first Pokémon game was released in 1996, Pokémon has been one of Nintendo's most successful franchises with over 380 million copies sold. As more consoles have released, and Pokémon continues to grow as a franchise, one thing remains constant: a Pokémon competitive scene.

Competitive Pokémon battling first came to life in the early 2000s, though it was still a relatively new concept. The competitions stayed in small circles until 2009, when The Pokémon Company held a World Championship for the first time. Ever since that, competitive Pokémon battle has exploded, allowing players to win cash prizes even up to $10,000. The competitive scene stays exciting with more and more Pokémon being brought into the fold with the release of each new generation, and clever players are always shaking up the game with new strategies. What started as a humble 151 Pokémon, has now ballooned to a huge 905, a number expected to increase with the new generation on the horizon.

Unfortunately, not all Pokémon are as good as others in the competitive scene. Type, stats, abilities, and a numerous other factors go in to determining a Pokémon's viability. Pokémon Showdown, an online competitive battle simulator made by Smogon, ranks each Pokémon in the following tiers determined by how often they are used in battle: Ubers, Overused, Underused, Rarely Used, Never Used, and Partially Used. There are also tiers in between them for Pokémon who are considered in between tiers. But with new Pokémon on the horizon, how will we be able to determine their viability, since they don't have usage stats yet? To do this, I need the answer the following question: "what makes a Pokémon good?" With that information, I will try to build a model to predict what tier a new Pokémon would be placed in, keeping a step ahead of the rest of the competitive scene.

It makes sense that Pokémon with a higher stat total, and thus stronger over all, would be in higher tiers. To test if this is true, I make a violin plot showing the distribution of stat totals for each tier. I also plot a regression line to show any relation.

From the plot, we can see that there is a relationship between the total stat distribution and the tier. As expected, the lower the stat total, the lower the tier. However, this relationship is clearly not sufficient for determining a Pokémon's tier. There is a huge range of stat totals for each tier. Ubers roughly has anywhere from 500-800 total stats, which is quite a wide range. Even the lowest tier, PU, can have up to almost 700 total stat.

So if stat total isn't sufficient for determining a Pokémon's tier, what else could be? I decided to turn my focus to types. Types can have all sorts of offensive and defensive advantages, weighing almost as heavily as a Pokémon's stat total in some cases. To show a relationship between type and tier, for each tier, a type's "score" goes up by 1 divided by the tier number. This gives heavier weight types found in higher tiers. I then standardize it by dividing by the total number of Pokémon with that type. I further normalize it by dividing by the max value, to give a range of values between 0 and 1.

scores = defaultdict(lambda: 0)
count = defaultdict(lambda: 0)
for index, row in df.iterrows():
    scores[row["Type.1"]] += 1 / (row["Tier_Num"] * 1.0)
    count[row["Type.1"]] += 1
    
    if row["Type.2"] != "None":
        scores[row["Type.2"]] += 1 / (row["Tier_Num"] * 1.0)
        count[row["Type.2"]] += 1

for key in scores:
    scores[key] = scores[key] / count[key]
    
maximum = max(scores.values())
minimum = min(scores.values())
for key in scores:
    scores[key] = (scores[key] - minimum) / (maximum - minimum)
    
scores = dict(sorted(scores.items(), key=lambda item: item[1], reverse=True))

Another interesting metric would be to consider how many weaknesses and strengths on average Pokémon of each tier have. In order to calculate strengths and weaknesses, I use this type chart, and read it into a pandas dataframe. A supereffective matchup is represented by a value of 2.0. Pokémon with multiple types multiply their values together to create the weakness modifier. I count a 4x weakness twice, since it should weigh heavier than a regular 2x weakness.

    weaknesses = 0
    num_pokemon = 0
    mask = df['Tier'] == tier

        if pokemon['Type.2'] == "None":
            for type in types:
                if type_chart.loc[pokemon['Type.1'], type] > 1:
                    strengths += 1
                if type_chart.loc[type, pokemon['Type.1']] > 1:
                    weaknesses += 1
        else:
            for type in types:
                if type_chart.loc[pokemon['Type.1'], type] > 1 or type_chart.loc[pokemon['Type.2'], type] > 1:
                    strengths += 1
                if type_chart.loc[type, pokemon['Type.1']] * type_chart.loc[type, pokemon['Type.2']] > 1:
                    weaknesses += type_chart.loc[type, pokemon['Type.2']] * type_chart.loc[type, pokemon['Type.2']] / 2
        
        num_pokemon += 1
        
    tier_matchup_data[tier] = [strengths / num_pokemon, weaknesses / num_pokemon]

Making Predictions¶

Ultimately, the goal is to be able to predict what tier a new Pokémon will fall in. To do this, I first test out how a Logistic Regression Classifier model will be able to predict the tier. To convert the type into numerical data that can be used by a Logistic Regression Classifier, I seperate each type into its own column and have it be a binary 1 or 0 to indicate presence of the type.

Now, I fit a Logistic Regression Classifier on the data. In order to ensure that I was fairly testing the accuracy of the model, I split the data into training and testing data using sklearn's built in train_test_split function. I also make use of sklearn's make_pipeline function, to build a pipeline that first scales the data, then sends it to the Logistic Regression Classifier. This allows for the model to fit more easily on the data, since it is standardized.

I noticed that the score was pretty low. I realized that this was because the scoring metric I was using was scoring 1 for an accurate answer, and 0 for anything else. However, if the guessed tier is close to the actual tier, it should be assigning a higher score than 0. To solve this problem, I implemented my own scoring function, where each prediction is weighted 1 if it is correct, and 3 / (3 + the difference in tiers) otherwise. I found that this had a good balancing in still giving points to tiers that were close, but with greatly diminished returns.

In [240]:
def custom_scorer(ground_truth, predictions):
    ground_truth = ground_truth.to_numpy()
    score = 0
    for i in range (0, len(predictions)):
        if tier_to_num[ground_truth[i]] == tier_to_num[predictions[i]]:
            score += 1
        else:
            score += 3 / (3 + abs(tier_to_num[ground_truth[i]] - tier_to_num[predictions[i]]))
    
    return score * 1.0 / len(predictions)

With the new scorer implemented, all I have to do is make it into a scoring function through sklearn's make_scorer function, and the refit the data on the pipeline.

This will have to wait until new Pokémon are released, though.

Conclusion¶

After training a Logistic Regression Classifier on the data, I determined that total base stats, as well as typing, do both have significant impact on the tier that a Pokémon will fall in. However, there are many other aspects that make a Pokémon "good."

Type and stats are simple to train a model on because stats are numeric and types are limited to certain values, but there are a number of other values that aren't so easily quantifiable for the sake of training a model that have weight on how well a Pokémon does. Abilities, held items, and moves, all have equal, if not greater in some circumstances, weight compared to stats and type. But Pokémon each can learn tens of moves, have hundreds of held items, and even multiple potential abilities sometimes. It is impossible to decide through code which is the best of those thousands of possibilities, and how that might be applied to another Pokémon. Take for example Mega Kangaskhan, an Uber Pokémon who sports unimpressive stats. However, it has an ability that allows it to hit twice in one turn, which skyrockets an otherwise average Pokémon to Ubers. Taking into account all these optimal abilities and held items would be a much greater scale than I could reasonably hope to accomplish in this project.

That being said, abilities, moves, and held items can only do so much for a Pokémon. While not the most accurate, measuring a Pokémon's "goodness" by stats and typing does a fairly good job in most cases. Besides for a couple of stragglers who were put in different tiers because of their ability or optimal movesets, the model was able to decently accurately predict most Pokémon. As different generations are released and "optimal" changes, types and stats will never change. For this reason, I consider this model successful.
